    <h2 class="wtf-title mt-7 mb-2 text-2xl text-purple-900">
      first, why Go was the hard one
    </h2>
    <p>
      <b>Go</b> is a 2,500-year-old board game from East Asia. Two players take
      turns placing black and white stones on a 19x19 grid, trying to surround
      territory. The rules fit on an index card. The game does not.
    </p>

    <p>
      Here is the number that kept computer scientists awake. The count of legal
      Go positions is roughly <b>10^170</b>. That is a 1 followed by 170 zeros.
      There are estimated to be about 10^80 atoms in the observable universe, so
      Go has more legal board states than there are atoms in the universe,
      squared, with room to spare. A pelican cannot picture that number. Neither
      can you. Nobody can, and that is exactly the point.
    </p>
    <p class="mt-3">
      Chess fell to this kind of math. In <b>1997</b>, IBM&apos;s <b>Deep
      Blue</b> beat world champion Garry Kasparov, and it largely did so by
      brute force: chess has a small enough branching factor (roughly 35 moves
      per turn) that a big enough machine can search millions of positions per
      second and just look further ahead than a human can. Deep Blue did not
      understand chess. It out-counted Kasparov.
    </p>
    <p class="mt-3">
      That trick does not work on Go. Go offers around <b>250 legal moves</b> on
      a typical turn, and games run hundreds of moves long. The search tree
      explodes so fast that even a planet-sized computer cannot count its way to
      the end. Worse, in Go there is no cheap way to glance at a position and
      score who is winning; strong play depends on a feel for shape, influence,
      and balance that top players describe in words like "thick" and "light."
      Pros said the best moves came from <i>intuition</i>. For decades, that
      was a polite way of saying: good luck programming this. As late as 2015,
      experts guessed a machine beating a top human at Go was at least a decade
      off.
    </p>

    <h2 class="wtf-title mt-7 mb-2 text-2xl text-purple-900">
      enter AlphaGo
    </h2>
    <p>
      <b>AlphaGo</b> was built by <b>DeepMind</b>, the London AI lab co-founded
      by <b>Demis Hassabis</b> (a chess prodigy and neuroscientist who would go
      on to share the 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for AlphaFold) and acquired
      by Google in 2014. Instead of brute-forcing the whole tree, it combined
      three ideas. It used <b>deep neural networks</b> (the same family of math
      as our pelican) to learn, from millions of human games, an intuition for
      which moves looked promising and which positions looked winning. It used{" "}
      <b>Monte Carlo tree search</b> to spend its limited search budget only on
      the moves that intuition flagged as worth reading. And then it used{" "}
      <b>reinforcement learning</b>, playing against versions of itself over and
      over (the same RL idea you met in the{" "}
      <a href="/pelicanplus/training" class="text-blue-700 underline">training lesson</a>),
      to get better than any human game could teach it.
    </p>
    <p class="mt-3">
      In <b>October 2015</b>, AlphaGo quietly beat the European champion{" "}
      <b>Fan Hui</b>, five games to zero. It was the first time a program had
      beaten a professional Go player on a full board with no handicap. The Go
      world was skeptical: Fan Hui, a fine player, was not in the top global
      tier. So DeepMind aimed higher.
    </p>

    <h2 class="wtf-title mt-7 mb-2 text-2xl text-purple-900">
      Seoul, March 2016: the match the world watched
    </h2>
    <p>
      In March 2016, in Seoul, AlphaGo faced <b>Lee Sedol</b>, a legend of the
      game, winner of 18 world titles, the kind of player other professionals
      study. The match was best of five, with a $1 million prize. Lee was so
      confident he predicted a 5-0 or 4-1 win for himself. An estimated 200
      million people watched.
    </p>
    <p class="mt-3">
      AlphaGo won the first game. Then the second game produced the moment this
      whole lesson is built around.
    </p>

    <div class="go-move my-4">
      <span class="go-move-label">Game 2 :: Move 37</span>
      <p style="margin:0;">
        On the 37th move, AlphaGo played a stone on the fifth line in a spot no
        professional would seriously consider that early. Commentators
        (themselves strong pros) assumed it was a bug. Fan Hui, watching, said
        it was "not a human move," and meant it as the highest compliment.
        AlphaGo&apos;s own estimate was that a human would have played that move
        with a probability of about <b>1 in 10,000</b>. It was not a mistake.
        Dozens of moves later, that stone was quietly running the whole board.
        It was the moment the machine showed it had not just memorized human Go;
        it had found <i>new</i> Go.
      </p>
    </div>

    <h2 class="wtf-title mt-7 mb-2 text-2xl text-purple-900">
      the one game a human won (Move 78, "the hand of God")
    </h2>
    <p>
      The story is not "machine flawless, humans obsolete," and the reason is
      Game 4. Down 0-3 and playing for pride, Lee Sedol found, on move 78, a
      stunning wedge between two white groups, a move so precise it has been
      nicknamed <b>"the hand of God."</b> It was, in its own way, AlphaGo&apos;s
      Move 37 in reverse: a brilliancy the machine had rated as wildly unlikely
      (again roughly 1 in 10,000). AlphaGo, blindsided, began to unravel,
      played a string of weak moves, and lost.
    </p>
    <p class="mt-3">
      Lee Sedol won Game 4. As of today it remains one of the very last times a
      top human beat a top Go AI under tournament conditions. He played one
      perfect move against the future and got one game back. The pelican
      salutes him.
    </p>

    <h2 class="wtf-title mt-7 mb-2 text-2xl text-purple-900">
      what came next (briefly, because it gets wild)
    </h2>
    <p>
      AlphaGo learned its intuition from human games. The successors threw the
      humans out entirely:
    </p>
    <ul class="mt-3 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
      <li>
        <b>AlphaGo Zero (2017)</b> learned Go from <i>zero</i> human games,
        starting from only the rules and playing itself. In three days it
        surpassed the version that beat Lee Sedol, and it beat that version{" "}
        <b>100 games to 0</b>. It rediscovered centuries of human Go theory in
        days, then went past it.
      </li>
      <li>
        <b>AlphaZero (2017)</b> generalized the same self-play recipe to{" "}
        <b>chess and shogi</b> too. One algorithm, no game-specific knowledge,
        superhuman at three different games. It learned chess in hours and
        played it in a style human grandmasters called alien and gorgeous.
      </li>
      <li>
        <b>MuZero (2019)</b> dropped the last crutch: it was not even told the
        rules. It learned a model of how each game works <i>and</i> how to win,
        purely by playing, and matched AlphaZero on Go, chess, and shogi while
        also crushing Atari video games.
      </li>
    </ul>
    <p class="mt-3">
      The trajectory in four years: from "learns from human experts" to "needs
      no humans" to "is not even told the rules." A pelican finds this both
      thrilling and a little bit of a reason to keep one eye open while
      sleeping.
    </p>

    <h2 class="wtf-title mt-7 mb-2 text-2xl text-purple-900">
      the short history this all fits into
    </h2>
    <p>
      AlphaGo is one beat in a longer drum. The honest one-sentence history of
      modern AI is: <b>a series of things people swore machines could never do,
      until they did.</b> The rail:
    </p>

    <ul class="go-rail">
      <li>
        <span class="go-year">1997</span>
        <b>Deep Blue beats Kasparov at chess.</b> Mostly brute-force search.
        Proof that "machines cannot play chess" was wrong, and a hint that
        raw counting would not be enough for the bigger games.
      </li>
      <li>
        <span class="go-year">2012</span>
        <b>The deep learning revolution (AlexNet).</b> A neural network called
        AlexNet, built by <b>Alex Krizhevsky</b>, <b>Ilya Sutskever</b>, and{" "}
        <b>Geoffrey Hinton</b>, crushed the ImageNet image-recognition contest
        on a pair of gaming GPUs. ImageNet itself was the doing of{" "}
        <b>Fei-Fei Li</b>, who spent years assembling the giant labeled dataset
        that made the contest possible. Suddenly neural nets, an old idea,
        actually worked at scale. This is the spark that lit everything after
        it, AlphaGo and your chatbot included.
      </li>
      <li>
        <span class="go-year">2016</span>
        <b>AlphaGo beats Lee Sedol.</b> Intuition (neural nets) plus planning
        (tree search) plus self-play (reinforcement learning). Proof that
        machines could do the "soft," judgment-heavy thing, not just the
        countable thing.
      </li>
      <li>
        <span class="go-year">2017</span>
        <b>The Transformer ("Attention Is All You Need").</b>{" "}
        <b>Ashish Vaswani</b> and a team at Google published a new
        neural-network architecture for handling sequences. It is the literal{" "}
        <b>T in GPT</b>. Every large language model on this site, including the
        ones drawing the pelicans, is a descendant of this paper.
      </li>
      <li>
        <span class="go-year">2018+</span>
        <b>Modern LLMs.</b> Scale the Transformer up, feed it most of the
        internet (the{" "}
        <a href="/pelicanplus/training" class="text-blue-700 underline">training lesson</a>),
        and you get GPT, Claude, Gemini, and the rest. OpenAI&apos;s GPT line
        (<b>Alec Radford</b> and <b>Ilya Sutskever</b> among the early authors)
        ran the Transformer-plus-scale playbook from GPT-1 in 2018 to the
        ChatGPT moment in 2022. The bird you are talking to is the
        great-grandchild of the machine that played Move 37.
      </li>
    </ul>

    <p class="mt-4">
      Notice the throughline. Each leap was preceded by confident experts
      explaining why it was impossible or decades away, and each one arrived
      anyway, usually faster than the safe estimate. That is the single most
      useful pattern to carry out of this school: in AI, "machines will never do
      X" has a poor track record, and "that is at least ten years off" has an
      even worse one.
    </p>

    <h2 class="wtf-title mt-7 mb-2 text-2xl text-purple-900">
      the pelican&apos;s takeaway
    </h2>
    <p>
      AlphaGo matters here for two reasons. First, it is where the field proved
      that the same basic ingredients, big neural networks plus lots of
      self-training, could produce something that looks like intuition and even
      creativity. Your pelican-drawing chatbot runs on a different architecture
      (the Transformer, not tree search), but it inherited the lesson: scale
      and learning beat hand-written rules. Second, Move 37 and Move 78 together
      are the whole emotional arc of this technology in two stones. The machine
      can find things no human would (37). A human can still, on the right day,
      find one thing the machine missed (78). And the gap between those two
      stones has only widened since.
    </p>
    <p class="mt-3">
      In <b>2019</b>, Lee Sedol retired from professional Go. He said that even
      if he became number one, there was now an entity that, in his words,
      <b> "cannot be defeated."</b> A pelican does not have a tidy joke for that
      one. Sometimes the bird just sits with the board for a while. SQUAWK,
      quietly.
    </p>
    <DrBeakman img="dr-beakman-4">
      every time experts swore &ldquo;machines never will&rdquo; or &ldquo;decades
      off,&rdquo; the machine showed up early. that is the one pattern to keep.
    </DrBeakman>

    <p class="mt-3">
      That is the origin story. From here we go back to the bird in front of you,
      the Transformer&apos;s great-grandchild, and start opening it up: first its
      tiny, leaky working memory.
    </p>
