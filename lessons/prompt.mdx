    <h2 class="wtf-title mt-7 mb-2 text-2xl text-purple-900">first, context</h2>
    <p>
      You already know the bird&apos;s working memory from the{" "}
      <a href="/pelicanplus/context-window" class="text-blue-700 underline">context window</a>{" "}
      lesson: immediate, precise, wiped every new chat. So treat the model as a{" "}
      <b>brilliant pelican-drawing intern with total amnesia</b> you met in an
      elevator. It knows nothing about your company, your codebase, or what you
      said thirty seconds ago. Every conversation starts in a blank wetland.
    </p>
    <p class="mt-3">
      <b>Context engineering</b> is the discipline of placing the right
      information in front of the model at the right moment: relevant documents,
      a worked example or two, the constraints that actually matter. Andrej
      Karpathy named it in 2025; Simon Willison explained why the rename
      mattered. &ldquo;Prompt engineering&rdquo; had been colonized by Twitter
      threads about magic words and jailbreak tricks. Context engineering points
      at the actual craft: what goes in the window, in what order, and why.
      (The other one has a LinkedIn certification. SQUAWK.)
    </p>
    <p class="mt-3">
      The flip side, also from that lesson: more is not better. Irrelevant tokens
      <i>distract</i> the bird and lower accuracy (the &ldquo;lost in the
      middle&rdquo; effect, extremely relatable if you have sat in a long
      meeting). The optimal context is curated: short, on-topic, no twelve
      tangents. This is why &ldquo;new chat&rdquo; is a power move. The bird does
      not miss the old conversation. It never knew anything was anywhere.
    </p>

    <h2 class="wtf-title mt-7 mb-2 text-2xl text-purple-900">then, the prompt</h2>
    <p>
      Once the bird has its habitat, you must ask. Clearly. Mumble at the model
      and it will give you a mumbled pelican. I learned this the hard way. I
      also learned it the other hard way, which is getting laid off.
    </p>
    <p class="mt-3">
      And remember the{" "}
      <a href="/pelicanplus/reasoning" class="text-blue-700 underline">reasoning</a>{" "}
      lesson: each token does only a sliver of computation, so demanding a
      one-word answer to a hard question crams all the thinking into a single
      forward pass it cannot afford. Give the bird runway. Let it lay out steps
      out loud. Visionaries reflect. Then they act. Then they write a substack
      about it.
    </p>

    <h2 class="wtf-title mt-7 mb-2 text-2xl text-purple-900">what wins (best practices)</h2>
    <ul class="ml-5 list-disc space-y-2">
      <li>
        <b>Be specific about outcome and format.</b> &ldquo;A side-view SVG,
        one pelican, one bicycle&rdquo; beats &ldquo;draw a bird, surprise me.&rdquo;
      </li>
      <li>
        <b>Show, don&apos;t just tell.</b> One or two examples of the ideal
        output (<b>few-shot</b>) is worth a thousand adjectives. This is not folk
        wisdom: it is the headline result of the GPT-3 paper (Brown et al., 2020),
        which showed a big enough model learns a new task just from examples in
        the prompt, no retraining. Prompting became a craft the day that worked.
      </li>
      <li>
        <b>Give the model room to think.</b> Chain of thought is not a trick;
        it is the bird doing its job correctly.
      </li>
      <li>
        <b>Feed facts in rather than trusting recall.</b> If accuracy matters,
        put the source in the window.
      </li>
      <li>
        <b>Front-load the context that matters</b> and cut the noise.
      </li>
      <li>
        <b>Iterate ruthlessly.</b> The first pelican is a draft.
      </li>
    </ul>

    <h2 class="wtf-title mt-7 mb-2 text-2xl text-purple-900">
      what we do not do here (anti-patterns)
    </h2>
    <ul class="ml-5 list-disc space-y-2">
      <li>
        <b>&ldquo;Make it good.&rdquo;</b> Not a vision. A shrug. The model
        will shrug back with many feathers.
      </li>
      <li>
        <b>Bribing or threatening the bird.</b> &ldquo;I&apos;ll tip you
        $200.&rdquo; Folklore. Lead with clarity instead. Or tip me. I have
        bills.
      </li>
      <li>
        <b>Contradicting yourself.</b> &ldquo;Be exhaustive but keep it to
        one line.&rdquo; Pick a lane. The bird will pick the worse one.
      </li>
      <li>
        <b>Burying the ask</b> in paragraph nine. If it matters, it goes first.
      </li>
      <li>
        <b>Assuming it remembers.</b> The intern has amnesia. Lovable amnesia,
        but amnesia.
      </li>
      <li>
        <b>Dumping the whole codebase in</b> and hoping for the best. Context
        engineering means <i>curating</i>. Junk in, junk out, just slower.
      </li>
    </ul>

    <h2 class="wtf-title mt-7 mb-2 text-2xl text-purple-900">
      the dial we refuse to touch (temperature &amp; variability)
    </h2>
    <p>
      Every time the bird picks its next token, it samples from a probability
      distribution over every word it has ever learned. The dial on that
      distribution is <b>temperature</b>. Turn it down toward zero and the
      model becomes a disciplined executive: focused, repeatable, a little
      boring. Turn it up and it becomes a poet at a wine tasting: expressive,
      surprising, occasionally a war crime.
    </p>
    <p class="mt-3">
      Its cousins, <b>top-p</b> (nucleus sampling) and <b>top-k</b>, decide
      how wide a pool of candidates the model may consider. top-p: only tokens
      whose cumulative probability adds up to P%. top-k: only the top K
      candidates, full stop. A newer method, <b>min-p</b>, scales the cutoff
      dynamically. The field keeps inventing new dials. You are probably fine
      with just temperature.
    </p>
    <p class="mt-3">
      This is why the same model, handed the identical prompt, nests two
      completely different pelicans on two different mornings. That is
      variability, and variability is where the magic and the horror both live.
      On the homepage we touch none of these dials. Factory default, sample
      freely. The pelican you see is the one they made, not one we tuned into
      looking good. Anyone can crank the temperature until something pretty
      falls out. We would rather show you the factory bird. Beaks and all.
    </p>

    <h2 class="wtf-title mt-7 mb-2 text-2xl text-purple-900">the mission</h2>
    <p>
      The benchmark on the homepage, one naive prompt, zero context, zero
      sampling params, is the opposite of everything I just told you. That is
      intentional. It measures the bird, not the operator. A raw capability
      signal: what can this model do, alone, with nothing? A humble question.
      Also, clearly, a hilarious question.
    </p>
    <p class="mt-3">
      But you are an operator. Curate your wetland, craft your covenant, give
      the bird room to think. Context engineering is not about tricking the
      bird. It is about giving it everything it needs to do the job you
      actually want done. The bird wants to help. It was trained to want this.
      SQUAWK.
    </p>
