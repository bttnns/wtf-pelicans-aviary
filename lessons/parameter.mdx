    <h2 class="wtf-title mt-7 mb-2 text-2xl text-purple-900">the two files, concretely</h2>
    <p>
      Take Llama 2 70B, the model Andrej Karpathy walks through in his intro
      talk. Seventy billion parameters, each stored as a 2-byte float: a
      <b>140 GB parameters file.</b> The inference code that runs it is roughly
      500 lines of C with no external dependencies. Put both on a laptop, compile
      once, and you have a full conversation with no internet, no subscription,
      no lab watching. The bird is yours.
    </p>
    <p class="mt-3">
      By 2026 the open-weights ecosystem pushed further: Meta&apos;s Llama 4
      Scout packs a <b>10-million-token context window</b> into a 109-billion-parameter
      mixture-of-experts model that fits on a single H100 GPU (only 17 billion
      parameters fire per token). The weights keep getting more capable per gigabyte.
    </p>

    <h2 class="wtf-title mt-7 mb-2 text-2xl text-purple-900">a lossy zip of the internet</h2>
    <p>
      Training ingested roughly <b>2 trillion tokens of text</b> (for Llama 2;
      modern frontier runs go much higher) and spent months nudging 70 billion
      little dials until the model could predict the next token accurately. Think
      of the parameters as a <b>zip file of the internet</b>, compressed about
      100x, but <i>lossy</i>, like a JPEG, not lossless.
    </p>
    <p class="mt-3">
      You get the gestalt: the shape of facts, the idioms, the vibes. Not a
      verbatim copy. The bird knows roughly what an ISBN looks like, which is
      exactly why it can hallucinate a convincing one. The pouch holds the shape
      of every fish it has eaten, not the fish themselves.
    </p>

    <h2 class="wtf-title mt-7 mb-2 text-2xl text-purple-900">nobody set the dials by hand</h2>
    <p>
      Nobody sat in a cubicle typing values into{" "}
      <i>what-a-pelican-looks-like.csv</i>. Training set them automatically:
      feed in text, predict the next token, compare to reality, nudge the dials,
      repeat, trillions of times.
    </p>
    <p class="mt-3">
      The Llama 2 70B run used roughly <b>1.7 million GPU-hours</b> on A100s.
      Cloud cost estimates range from <b>$2 million to $8 million</b> (Meta got
      a bulk discount; they did). Frontier models in 2025-2026 cost orders of
      magnitude more. This is why &ldquo;just retrain it&rdquo; is not a weekend
      project and why your landlord is not building a GPT-5 competitor in his
      garage, no matter what the podcast says.
    </p>

    <h2 class="wtf-title mt-7 mb-2 text-2xl text-purple-900">bigger is (reliably) smarter, to a point</h2>
    <p>
      The idea that piling on more dials would pay off is not new. Geoffrey
      Hinton and his students lit the fuse in 2012, when their deep network
      AlexNet won the ImageNet contest (Fei-Fei Li&apos;s benchmark) by a
      humiliating margin and convinced everyone that bigger, deeper, hungrier
      networks were the way forward. Language models inherited that lesson. The
      spooky thing about parameters is how <i>boring</i> the scaling law turned
      out to be: next-token accuracy is a smooth, predictable function of{" "}
      <b>N (parameters) and D (training tokens)</b>. More dials plus more data
      equals a reliably better bird. This is why the labs kept shipping models
      with names that are just bigger numbers.
    </p>
    <p class="mt-3">
      By 2025-2026, distillation and mixture-of-experts (only a fraction of dials
      fire per token) deliver GPT-4-era performance from a model an order of
      magnitude smaller. The dials got cheaper per unit of smart. The flock got
      denser. The venture capitalists got louder.
    </p>

    <h2 class="wtf-title mt-7 mb-2 text-2xl text-purple-900">why you should care</h2>
    <p>
      &ldquo;The model&rdquo; is not a mysterious oracle; it is a matrix
      multiplication your laptop can do if you have the file. When a lab says
      they are &ldquo;improving the model,&rdquo; they mean: a training job
      produced a different set of dials. When they say the model
      &ldquo;knows&rdquo; something, they mean: it was compressed, lossily, into
      the dial positions. The bird is the dials. And nobody set them: the next
      lesson is the strange, expensive process that did. SQUAWK.
    </p>
