    <h2 class="wtf-title mt-7 mb-2 text-2xl text-purple-900">
      it writes a picture as text
    </h2>
    <p>
      An SVG is not a photo. It is <b>code</b>: a list of instructions like
      &ldquo;draw a line from here to there,&rdquo; &ldquo;put a circle at this
      spot,&rdquo; &ldquo;curve a path along these points.&rdquo; The model
      writes that code the only way it writes anything: one token at a time, top
      to bottom, left to right, the way I am typing this sentence and the way it
      cannot un-type the last word.
    </p>
    <p class="mt-3">
      So every <code>&lt;line&gt;</code>, every <code>&lt;path&gt;</code>, every
      <code>&lt;circle&gt;</code> is <b>one brush stroke</b>. The bird commits to
      it. There is no undo. There is no step back from the easel. There is no
      glance at the canvas to check how it is going. It lays down a stroke,
      forgets the brush was ever wet, and reaches for the next one.
    </p>

    <LessonArt
      id="flying-blind-2"
      caption="the very same idea from a lazy four-word prompt, drawn by a different model. click it to read the prompt, and notice how much the bird had to guess."

    <h2 class="wtf-title mt-7 mb-2 text-2xl text-purple-900">
      no eyes on its own work
    </h2>
    <p>
      You, a human with a head full of eyeballs, draw a pelican by looking. You
      sketch a beak, you squint, the beak is too big, you fix the beak. The
      feedback loop IS the drawing.
    </p>
    <p class="mt-3">
      The model has no loop. It cannot tell whether its pelican came out looking
      like a pelican or like a wet sock or like a confused flamingo having a
      crisis. It has <b>no eyes on its own output.</b> It picks a number for
      where the wheel goes, picks a number for where the body goes, picks a
      number for the beak, and prays to a god it also cannot see. The first time
      <i>anyone</i> sees the picture is when a browser renders the code. By then
      the bird has already flown off.
    </p>

    <h2 class="wtf-title mt-7 mb-2 text-2xl text-purple-900">
      so what is actually hard here
    </h2>
    <p>
      Writing valid markup is easy. Tags close, numbers parse, the file is legal
      SVG every time. That was never the test. The hard part is the thing you do
      without thinking: knowing that a bicycle has <b>two wheels with a frame
      between them</b>, that a body sits on that frame, that a beak attaches to a
      head and not a knee, that objects have volume and sit in <b>space</b> in
      relation to each other.
    </p>
    <p class="mt-3">
      That is a <b>world model</b>, and a pure next-token predictor was never
      handed one. It learned which words follow which words, blindfolded, from a
      mountain of text. Yann LeCun has been blunt about this for years: an
      autoregressive language model is missing the internal model of how the
      physical world is laid out, which is why he is off chasing
      &ldquo;world models&rdquo; instead. Fei-Fei Li calls the missing piece
      <b>spatial intelligence</b>: understanding 3D space, geometry, and
      physics, not just stringing symbols together. Both of them are pointing at
      the exact gap our blindfolded painter falls into.
    </p>

    <DrBeakman compact img="dr-beakman-4">
      valid markup is trivial. the hard part is the world model: knowing a beak
      attaches to a head, not a knee. that is spatial intelligence, and it was
      never handed one.
    </DrBeakman>

    <h2 class="wtf-title mt-7 mb-2 text-2xl text-purple-900">
      why the pelican is a real test
    </h2>
    <p>
      Now you see why &ldquo;draw a pelican on a bicycle&rdquo; is not a gimmick.
      It is a trap, and a beautiful one. The prompt forces the model to hold a
      whole little scene in its head, two wheels, a frame, a bird, a beak, the
      bird perched on the frame and not melted through it, and render it
      <b>blind, in one pass, with no eraser.</b> There is nothing to memorize.
      There is no stock answer to copy. The model either has a sense of how
      objects sit in space or it does not, and the rendered picture tattles on it
      instantly.
    </p>
    <p class="mt-3">
      That is the whole reason this site exists. Every bird in the gallery is a
      blind painter turning in homework it has never been allowed to look at. Go
      judge the results. The bird sure couldn&apos;t. The capstone, the last
      lesson, is where all of this lands at once.
    </p>
