  <h2 class="wtf-title mt-7 mb-2 text-2xl text-purple-900">a token is a chunk, not a letter</h2>
  <p>
    A token is a <i>piece</i> of text: sometimes a whole short word, usually a
    fragment, sometimes punctuation or whitespace. The model has a fixed menu of
    them, called its <b>vocabulary</b>. Every bit of text you send gets minced
    into menu items before the bird tastes any of it.
  </p>
  <p class="mt-3">
    GPT-2 (2019) ate from a menu of about <b>50,000 tokens</b>. GPT-4 (2023)
    grew that to <b>100,000</b>. GPT-4o (2024) doubled it to roughly{" "}
    <b>200,000</b>. More menu, bigger bites: the same sentence becomes fewer,
    fatter tokens and the bird sees more text before its mouth is full. The pouch
    keeps getting roomier.
  </p>

  <h2 class="wtf-title mt-7 mb-2 text-2xl text-purple-900">where the menu comes from (BPE)</h2>
  <p>
    Nobody hand-wrote 200,000 tokens. The menu is grown by an algorithm with the
    gloriously unglamorous name <b>byte pair encoding</b>. Rico Sennrich and
    colleagues brought it into language modeling in 2016 to handle rare words;
    Alec Radford and the OpenAI team carried the same trick into the GPT line,
    which is why every model you talk to today still eats from a BPE menu. The
    recipe:
  </p>
  <ol class="mt-2 list-decimal space-y-1 pl-6">
    <li>Start with every character as its own tiny token.</li>
    <li>Find the two neighbors that appear together most often. Fuse them. Add to the menu.</li>
    <li>Repeat tens of thousands of times.</li>
  </ol>
  <p class="mt-3">
    Common pairs like &ldquo;th&rdquo; and &ldquo;ing&rdquo; fuse early. Rare
    combos stay as crumbs. Common English words become single tokens; unusual
    words get shredded into pieces; emoji become a whole adventure.
  </p>

  <h2 class="wtf-title mt-7 mb-2 text-2xl text-purple-900">
    the strawberry problem (and why it mostly got fixed)
  </h2>
  <p>
    For a few years every AI demo included the trick: ask a model how many r&apos;s
    are in <i>strawberry</i>. Early models said two. The reason was tokenization:
    &ldquo;strawberry&rdquo; got chopped into two or three tokens, letters inside
    a token pur&eacute;ed beyond recognition. You were asking the bird to count
    sprinkles blended into a smoothie. By 2024-2025 the labs patched this through
    larger vocabularies and reasoning-focused fine-tuning. Modern models usually
    get it right. But the lesson stands: a startling amount of model
    &ldquo;dumbness&rdquo; is tokenization having a moment. The blender is still
    running. It is just a fancier blender now.
  </p>

  <h2 class="wtf-title mt-7 mb-2 text-2xl text-purple-900">why you should care</h2>
  <p>
    Tokens are the unit of <i>everything</i> downstream. The model thinks in
    tokens, its memory is measured in tokens, and your bill, the one that
    replaced your salary, is counted in tokens. The bird is not reading. It is
    pattern-matching on a menu it memorized during training, and doing it
    frighteningly well for something that has never seen the alphabet.
  </p>
  <p class="mt-3">
    So: the bird eats tokens. Next question, the one the whole school turns on:
    where does it keep what it learned? The answer is a pile of numbers nobody
    set by hand. SQUAWK.
  </p>
